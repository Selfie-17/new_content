

# Story: Riya’s First Paycheck and the Three Boxes


Riya had just finished Class 12 and was spending her break interning at a local startup. On a Friday evening, her phone buzzed with the notification she had been waiting for.


**"Salary Credited: ₹8,000."**


She immediately screenshotted it and sent it to her family group.
**Dad:** "Congratulations!  But come home this weekend. We need to talk about investing, not just spending."


Riya groaned. She had just finished the chapter on **Probability** in her Maths textbook—Sample Spaces, Events, Mutually Exclusive, Exhaustive. She wanted to buy shoes, not discuss finance. She certainly didn't expect the two worlds to collide.


---


### Scene 1: The Experiment
On Sunday morning, Riya walked into the living room to find her dad sitting at the dining table. In front of him were three empty shoe boxes and a whiteboard.


"What is this?" Riya asked, grabbing an apple. "Magic tricks?"


"No," her dad smiled. "Portfolio management. You want to invest your salary, right?"


"Yes... but I'm scared. I don't want to lose my money."


"Good. Only scared people invest carefully." He handed her a stack of Monopoly money. "Imagine this is your salary. Investing is basically a **Random Experiment**."


Riya raised an eyebrow. "Like tossing a coin?"


"Exactly. The **Experiment** is 'The Market's Performance this Year'. We don't know the outcome yet. But we know the possibilities. That list of possibilities is your **Sample Space ($S$)**."


He pointed to the three boxes. He had labeled them with a marker:
1. **Box A:** Tech & AI (Software, Robots)
2. **Box B:** Old Economy (Steel, Oil, Banks)
3. **Box C:** Green Future (Solar, EV batteries)






"In probability," Dad said, "An **Event** is a subset of the sample space. Here, putting money in Box A is betting on the Event: *'Tech Sector performs well this year.'*"


---


### Scene 2: The "I Will Never Lose" Illusion (Exhaustive Events)


Riya looked at the token in her hand. "Okay, so if I put all my money in Tech, and Tech crashes, I lose."


"Correct. That’s a Simple Event," Dad noted.


"That sounds risky," Riya said. She started dealing the money out. She put some notes in Tech, some in Old Economy, and some in Green Future. "There. Now I’m safe."


"Why?"


"Because," Riya reasoned, "The economy has to be *somewhere*, right? Either tech wins, or oil and banks win, or the new green stuff wins. If I bet on all of them, at least one part of my money will grow."


Her dad’s eyes lit up. He grabbed the whiteboard.


"Riya, you just defined **Exhaustive Events**."


He wrote:
$$A \cup B \cup C = S$$


"If these three boxes cover every possible way the market can grow, then they are **Exhaustive**. Their Union is the entire Sample Space."






"By diversifying," he explained, "You are mathematically ensuring that **at least one** of your events occurs. You have eliminated the risk of *everything* failing at once, assuming the world doesn't end."


---


### Scene 3: The Mutually Exclusive Trap


"Okay, I get it," Riya said, feeling confident. "So I just pick opposite things? Like, I'll bet on 'Market Goes Up' and 'Market Goes Down' at the same time?"


Her dad laughed. "And that brings us to the mistake most beginners make. The **Mutually Exclusive** trap."


He cleared the table and placed two new cards down:
1. **High-Risk Small Companies (Small Caps)**
2. **Safe Giant Companies (Large Caps)**


"Many people think," Dad started, "that if they buy risky stocks AND safe stocks, they will make a huge profit on both every year."


"Won't they?"


"Think about the events," Dad said.
* **Event X:** Investors are feeling brave and greedy (Small caps boom).
* **Event Y:** Investors are fearful and want safety (Large caps hold steady, small caps crash).


"Usually, looking at a single specific timeframe, extreme greed and extreme fear are **Mutually Exclusive**. They cannot happen at the same instant."


He drew two separate circles on the board that didn't touch.
$$X \cap Y = \varnothing$$






"You cannot force two mutually exclusive events to both be 'Winners' simultaneously. If you bet on both expecting both to skyrocket together, you are fighting probability. Usually, when one wins, the other loses or stays flat."


---


### Scene 4: The Market Correction (The Intersection)


"So," Riya sat back, "Investing is just... Algebra of Events?"


"Pretty much," Dad nodded. "Imagine a year where the market crashes."


He drew a big circle called **"Correction"**.


"If you only hold **Event T (Tech)**, and the crash hits Tech hard, your event fails. You lose."


"But," he continued, "If you hold **Event T (Tech)** AND **Event F (FMCG - like soaps and biscuits)**..."


"People still buy soap when the market crashes," Riya realized.


"Exactly. The intersection of 'Market Crash' and 'Tech' might be bad. But the intersection of 'Market Crash' and 'Soap' is stable. By holding both, you ensure that the event 'My Portfolio Survives' remains true."


---


### Scene 5: The Realization


Riya looked at the boxes differently now. She didn't just see companies; she saw Venn diagrams.


"So," Riya murmured, "A random experiment is 'What will the market do?'. My job is to pick a set of events that are..."


She paused, looking for the word.


"...**Exhaustive** enough so that I don't get wiped out," she said. "But I shouldn't be stupid and expect **Mutually Exclusive** things to both make me rich at the same time."


Her dad capped the marker. "I think you're ready to open a Demat account."


Riya picked up a token. "I'll put this one in the 'Learning' box first. But Dad?"


"Yeah?"


"Probability is actually useful. Just don't tell my Maths teacher I admitted that."
